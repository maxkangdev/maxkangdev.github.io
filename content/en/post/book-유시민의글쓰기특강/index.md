As I wrote more blog posts, I realized just how terrible my writing skills were.  
While browsing through my E-book, I came across this book and started reading it right away.

Since I mostly read technical books, I had expected this book to cover writing techniques—things like structuring a piece, choosing the right vocabulary, and dividing paragraphs effectively.  
However, it doesn’t focus much on those aspects. Instead, it mainly discusses topics like the philosophy of writing, the aesthetics of argumentation, attitudes toward writing, and the necessity of writing itself.

One realization I had while reading: I am nowhere near ready to learn the techniques I initially sought.  
To write well, one must first build a strong vocabulary through years of reading and exposure to various types of writing. This helps distinguish good writing from bad.  
Only after that can one improve by consistently practicing writing.  
Since I haven’t read many books so far, my first step should be to read more. 😅😅😅

The chapter I liked the most was Chapter 7, *Writing is a Blessing*, which explains why writing (well) is important.  
Before reading this book, I wouldn’t have been able to answer the following question—or I would have dismissed it with, “Why does it even matter?”
> Why do you write?

But is the reason really that important? **Yes, it is.**  
In fact, having a reason is crucial not just for writing but for anything we do—it’s the source of motivation.

Have you ever started something without a clear reason? How long did you keep it up? Probably not very long.  
Think about a friend who barely studied in high school but suddenly started getting straight A’s in college.  
Or conversely, a top student in high school who struggled in college, failing courses or even dropping out.  
The difference? The former found a reason to study, while the latter lost theirs.

I once tried playing golf because my brother strongly recommended it. I stuck with it for about three months before quitting—it wasn’t fun, and it didn’t feel like exercise to me, so I had no reason to continue.  
On the other hand, I’ve been consistently going to the gym for years. After experiencing firsthand the importance of health in my youth, I found a clear reason to work out consistently.

Writing is the same. Without a proper reason, this blog—especially non-technical posts—wouldn’t have lasted more than two months.  
But through Chapter 7, I discovered why I need to keep writing.
> Writing is an act of self-expression.

I’m an engineering student and a textbook example of an "emotionally unavailable" type personality. That means I’m terrible at expressing emotions.  
I tend to suppress my inner feelings, thoughts, and desires.  
This lack of emotional expression sometimes makes social interactions difficult or even puts me at a disadvantage.  
Even though I recognize this, personality isn’t something that changes overnight.  
I’ve watched videos and read books on the topic, trying to improve.  
But the advice always boils down to the same things: *"Be empathetic,"* *"Make an effort to understand others,"* *"Practice expressing emotions."*  
I’ve seen this advice dozens of times. I know it all.  
Yet when the moment comes, I revert to my old ways.  
Can I even change? I sometimes wonder.

Writing gave me a glimmer of hope.  
I may struggle to express my emotions, thoughts, and desires through speech, but I can write them down.  
And once I put them into words on paper, I find that I can say them out loud—at least to some extent.  
Finally, I’ve found a method that works for me.

The book puts it this way:
> Our lives are filled with acts of self-expression.  
> If we fail to express our inner thoughts, emotions, and desires in a timely and proper manner, life becomes suffocating.  
> What’s inside each of us, and how we express it, determines the course of our lives.

**If writing has the power to change the course of my life, is there any reason not to keep doing it?**  


## Additionally  
Above, I shared my overall thoughts on the book. Below are some thoughts related to writing.  

### Writing Rules  
1. Distinguish between personal preferences and arguments.  
2. Always support arguments with reasoning.  
3. Stay focused on the main topic from beginning to end.  

Lastly, the most important thing is: **Read a lot and write a lot.**  
There are no shortcuts.
